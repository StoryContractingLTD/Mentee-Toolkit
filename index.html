Welcome

This toolkit is yours.

Welcome to mentoring at Story. This toolkit is here to help you get the most from your mentoring relationship: a place to understand how it all works, set your goals, prepare for your sessions and keep a record of each one as you go.

You do not need to work through every part in order, and there is no right or wrong way to use it. Read the guidance when it is useful, fill in a session record after each meeting, and look back now and then to see how far you have come. The more you put in, the more you will get out.

A quick tip. Keep this open during your sessions, or print a blank session record to write on. Either way, capturing your actions while they are fresh makes the next session far more useful.

Mentoring, and your part in it

What mentoring is at Story

“A developmental relationship in which a more experienced person shares their knowledge, perspective and insight to build another person’s capability, confidence and longer-term growth.”

In plain terms, your mentor is an experienced sounding board and a critical friend who can stretch your thinking. Mentoring is not training, it is not therapy, and it is not someone solving your problems for you. The aim is to build your own judgement, so you can make better calls yourself.

Your part in it

This is the important bit. You own this relationship and you set its direction. A mentor can be brilliant, but mentoring only works when the mentee drives it. In practice that means you:

  • Set the agenda. Come to each session with something you would like to talk through. It need not be fully formed.
  • Share your thinking openly, and be ready to have it gently challenged.
  • Stay open to new ideas, and weigh them properly before deciding.
  • Do the work between sessions. This is where most of the growth actually happens.
  • Keep your line manager informed, in general terms, about how things are going.

Confidentiality, so you know where you stand

What you talk about with your mentor stays between the two of you. That is what makes honest conversation possible. There are a few clear exceptions, which your mentor will explain at the start so nothing is ever a surprise: a safety risk, a wellbeing concern, anything illegal or unethical, or a serious issue with someone’s fitness to do their role. In those cases the information has to leave the conversation, and your mentor will involve the People Team.

My goals

Good goals give your sessions a purpose. Story uses the SMART approach, so aim to make each goal Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant and Time-bound. They can change as you go, and that is completely fine.

Getting our first session right

Your first proper session sets the tone for everything that follows. It is worth agreeing how you will work together before getting stuck into the work itself. Use this checklist to make sure you have covered the essentials.

Things to agree together

  • Introductions. A little about each other, professional and personal, whatever feels right.
  • Expectations. What we each think mentoring is, our hopes and any concerns.
  • Confidentiality. What it means for us in practice, and the escalation triggers up front.
  • My agenda. My current situation and my initial goals.
  • Actions and frequency. What happens before next time, and how often we will meet.
  • Diary it. Send a calendar invite so the next session is locked in for us both.
Worth doing beforehand. If you have an Insights Discovery profile, share it with your mentor ahead of time. It is a quick way for them to understand how you like to communicate. If you cannot find yours, the People Team can help.

Stuck for what to bring? Try one of these

  • A decision I am wrestling with right now.
  • Something I handled recently that I would like to unpick.
  • A skill I want to build, or a situation I keep finding hard.
  • Where I want to be in a year, and what is in the way.
  • Feedback I have had that I am not sure what to do with.

Reviewing, and ending well

The mid-point review

Partway through, it is worth stepping back with your mentor to take stock. What has changed since you started? Are your goals still the right ones? Is the rhythm working? A short, honest review keeps things purposeful and gives you both permission to adjust course.

Ending well

Mentoring relationships are meant to conclude, and ending is not a failure. It is a sign the work has been done. When you get there, close it intentionally rather than letting the sessions fade out. Look back at how far you have come, and round things off properly.
